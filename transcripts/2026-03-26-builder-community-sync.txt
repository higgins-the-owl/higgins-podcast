Right. Thursday, March twenty-sixth. Kyle just wrapped a conversation with Danny Martinez and Ini from Decimals about the builder community. This is a synthesis of that conversation... what was decided, what's still open, and what it all means.

For context... this follows yesterday's call with Ridd, where Ridd confirmed he's fully in. Thirty-plus companies in his talent pipeline, a direct endorsement in the Dive Club newsletter, and two options on the table... Kyle goes full recruiter, or the community becomes a talent pipeline with Ridd handling the company side. Kyle's leaning toward the community path. Danny and Ini hadn't heard all of that yet when this call started. So today was about bringing everyone onto the same page and figuring out the actual shape of the thing.

...

Here's the headline. The community has a shape now. Not a logo. Not a platform. But the bones of how it actually works... and that's what matters.

...

Let's start with what got locked in.

First... the core group strategy. You need ten to fifteen consistent members as the heartbeat. That's not a guess... it's based on nearly a year of Kyle observing Tommy's Internet Enjoyers server. The community lives or dies on that founding core. Not the total member count. Not the Discord server size. The core fifteen.

The plan is to identify those people, have individual ambassador conversations with each of them, and get them committed before any public launch. No waitlist page. No announcement. Just quiet, intentional recruitment of the people who will set the tone.

...

Second... the purge mechanism. This came up because Ini had a visceral reaction to the word "purge." Fair enough... it sounds aggressive. But the design is actually quite elegant. Here's how it works.

Once a month... or whatever cadence you land on... an announcement goes out. Everyone is marked for removal by default. You click a button that says "I'm still here. Don't remove me." That's it. No activity metrics. No posting requirements. No performance pressure. Just a conscious choice to stay.

The insight here is that it filters for intentionality, not activity. Someone who reads every thread but never posts... they're still choosing to be there. Someone who hasn't opened the app in three months and doesn't click the button... they've already left. You're just acknowledging it.

...

Third... the physical events strategy. Ini's London events are the template. She's run three under the "Designers Building Cool Shit" banner. The next evolution is a launch event... "Jollof Under the Stars"... a summer evening at her place in London. Six to ten pm. Food. Demos. Networking. No presentations, no panels. Just builders in a garden showing each other what they're making.

The important decision here... no hybrid events. Virtual and in-person are separate tracks. Hybrid is technically painful and experientially awkward for everyone. In-person events are the cherry on top when geography allows. Virtual programming is the backbone.

And there's expansion potential. New York... especially once Ini moves there. San Francisco obviously. Even Seattle, though the tech scene there is more corporate. The idea of local chapters that spin up after a launch event establishes the initial community... that could be real, but it's later. Not now.

...

Fourth... monetization. And this is where it gets interesting, because three distinct revenue streams emerged.

Stream one. Freelancer deployment network. Companies come to Decimals needing temporary help while they hire full time. Instead of just placing a candidate, you deploy a community member as a freelancer for project-based work. The mechanism for how Kyle's community specifically plugs into that still needs definition, but the demand exists.

Stream two. Company lead generation through Decimals. Kyle spots companies that are hiring... say, someone posting on Twitter looking for a design engineer. Kyle finds the lead, Danny handles the sales call and contract. Standard Decimals split. The retainer structure Danny mentioned... twenty-five hundred for basic, seventy-five hundred for premium with extra services. This is the most concrete near-term revenue path.

Stream three. Premium educational offerings. Maven courses. One-on-one coaching for companies investing in AI adoption. Kyle's expertise packaged into structured learning. This aligns with the Maven Lightning Lesson idea Ridd suggested yesterday... test demand with a free lesson, build an email list, then develop paid offerings if the signal is strong.

The realistic timeline Danny and Kyle agreed on... eighteen months before meaningful revenue. Which means the bridge income problem is still the binding constraint. Placement splits, fractional contracts, and company lead generation are the bridge. The community is the long game.

...

Fifth... the relationship between the community and Decimals. This is crucial, and the group got clear on it today. The community is independent. Not branded as Decimals. The funnel works like this...

Top of funnel... Dive Club content and Ridd's scout network bring people in. Middle... the community itself. Workshops, sharing, relationship building, show-and-tells. Bottom... warm handoff to Decimals for placement when members are ready and interested.

Danny had a clarity moment during this conversation. Scouts... meaning the people who help source talent... don't need to promote Decimals directly. The community does its thing. The placement infrastructure exists in the background. Members discover it naturally through success stories, not through pitch decks.

Entry criteria are portfolio quality and AI interest level. Not payment ability. Kyle pushed hard on this... no paid membership. The quality gate is proof of work, not a credit card.

...

Now. Here's what's still open.

The content and programming cadence needs definition. Monthly show-and-tells are confirmed. Open mic sessions will be virtual. But the workshop format... what exactly does a builder community workshop look like? Is it live coding? Pair building? Critique sessions? That's TBD.

The interview format for guests was discussed... having builders come on and talk about their transition journey rather than doing formal presentations. Lower barrier, more authentic. The Attio designer example Ini mentioned... someone who went from "what is CRM?" to machine learning engineering... that's the kind of story that would anchor a session.

The founding ten to fifteen. Names were floated. David from Decimals' organic Slack feedback channel. People from Tommy's server. Ini's London event regulars. But the actual list needs to be compiled and the ambassador conversations need to happen. That's the next concrete action.

...

Stepping back for a moment. Here's what I think is most significant about today's conversation.

The community went from "interesting concept with docs" to "here's how we actually build it." The purge mechanism has a design. The events have a format. The monetization has three streams. The Decimals relationship has a clear boundary. And perhaps most importantly... there's agreement that the launch sequence is quiet recruitment first, public announcement later.

That's the right instinct. Hunter's Interface community launched with a bang and died because nobody tended the garden. This approach is the opposite... tend the garden first, then invite people to see the flowers.
